\begin{abstract}
Quantitative trading systems can produce internally consistent backtests while
misstating investment performance through omitted cash returns, unmatched
benchmarks, hidden transaction-cost assumptions, or research and execution
paths that implement different semantics. We present an audit-first evaluation
of \system, an open-source, weight-centric research and paper-trading pipeline,
and use a fixed six-ETF rotation strategy as a deliberately untuned case study.
The audit formalizes residual-cash accounting, next-bar execution, mandatory
costs, point-in-time contracts, coherent execution state, and machine-readable
provenance. On 2,011 U.S. trading sessions from 2018--2025, residual cash earns
an SHV adjusted-close proxy and the strategy is compared with passive returns
scaled to the same daily risky exposure. The audited strategy earns a 1.33\%
net CAGR, a -0.14 cash-excess Sharpe ratio, and a -10.33\% maximum drawdown.
Before costs, it trails an exposure-matched equal-initial-weight buy-and-hold
basket by 3.08 percentage points per year in arithmetic return; a paired
circular-block bootstrap gives a 95\% interval of [-5.58\%, -0.56\%]. All four
predefined overlay variants trail their matched passive comparator, and the
conclusion persists across cost levels, subperiods, and two backtest engines.
The contribution is not a new alpha model; it is a reproducible negative result
and a concrete protocol for distinguishing software correctness from economic
evidence.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Modern quantitative platforms increasingly unify data processing, feature
construction, portfolio allocation, simulation, and broker execution
\citep{yang2020qlib,liu2020finrl,yang2026finrlx}. This integration reduces
friction, but it also makes accounting and interface assumptions load-bearing.
A strategy that is 40\% invested has a 60\% cash position whether or not a
backtest models it. A benchmark with 100\% risky exposure does not answer
whether security selection added value at 40\% exposure. Likewise, agreement
between a chart and a README does not establish causality, cost realism, or
execution safety.

These issues are especially consequential in financial machine learning,
where observations are dependent, effective sample sizes are small, and model
or strategy selection creates substantial false-discovery risk
\citep{white2000reality,harvey2016crosssection,bailey2014deflated,
bailey2017pbo,arnott2019protocol}. Reproducible software is necessary but not
sufficient: the reproduced quantity must itself be economically well-defined.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it specifies an auditable return
contract for weight-centric pipelines: causal target weights, explicit
residual cash, mandatory costs, aligned comparators, and separable asset, cash,
and cost contributions. Second, it describes engineering controls that bind
reported results to code, inputs, dependencies, and execution state. Third, it
reports a fixed negative-result study of a multi-asset rotation strategy. We
report all predefined variants rather than selecting a winner, and separate
gross signal quality from net implementability. The purpose is to show what
changes when the accounting question is posed correctly, not to claim a
profitable trading rule.

\section{Related work}

Portfolio construction originates from explicit trade-offs between return and
risk \citep{markowitz1952portfolio}; momentum evidence motivates ranking assets
by past returns across both securities and asset classes
\citep{jegadeesh1993returns,moskowitz2012timeseries}. The case-study strategy
uses these ideas only as a test vehicle. We do not claim that its particular
grouping, residualization, or overlays improve on that literature.

Open platforms such as Qlib and FinRL provide modular workflows for financial
AI research \citep{yang2020qlib,liu2020finrl}; FinRL-X emphasizes a common
weight interface across research and deployment \citep{yang2026finrlx}. Our
focus is narrower and complementary: the accounting and state invariants that
must hold when such an interface is evaluated or sent toward a broker.

Sharpe-ratio inference is sensitive to distributional and serial-dependence
assumptions \citep{lo2002statistics}. Data snooping and repeated trials further
inflate apparent performance \citep{white2000reality,bailey2014deflated,
bailey2017pbo}. We therefore avoid selecting among variants and use paired
block resampling for uncertainty. More broadly, the manifest, locked
environment, and executable protocol follow the reproducibility goals described
by \citet{pineau2021reproducibility}.

\section{Audit protocol}

\subsection{Weight, timing, and cash contracts}

Let $w_t\in\mathbb{R}^N$ be the risky-asset target decided after observing the
close at date $t$. In the audited convention, the target executes on the first
bar strictly after $t$ and begins earning returns only after that execution
bar. Thus close-derived signals cannot earn the same close they observe. For
the long-only case, $w_{i,t}\geq 0$ and $\sum_i w_{i,t}\leq 1$; the residual
cash weight is
\begin{equation}
  w^c_t = 1-\sum_{i=1}^N w_{i,t}.
\end{equation}
For asset returns $r_{i,t}$ and cash-account return $r^c_t$, pre-cost portfolio
return is
\begin{equation}
  r^{\mathrm{gross}}_t = \sum_{i=1}^N w_{i,t-1}r_{i,t}
  + w^c_{t-1}r^c_t.
  \label{eq:gross}
\end{equation}
Missing cash observations fail the run instead of silently becoming zero. The
after-cost return is $r^{\mathrm{net}}_t=r^{\mathrm{gross}}_t-c_t$. The baseline
uses a symmetric 13 basis-point charge per dollar traded with no commission or
tax decomposition. This flat model is a first-order sensitivity assumption,
not a market-impact model; large or urgent orders require size- and
liquidity-aware execution models such as \citet{almgren2001execution}.

We report nominal CAGR, maximum drawdown, annualized one-way turnover, and
cash-excess Sharpe
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{SR}_c = \sqrt{252}\,
  \frac{\operatorname{mean}(r_t-r^c_t)}
       {\operatorname{sd}(r_t-r^c_t)}.
  \label{eq:sharpe}
\end{equation}
An SHV adjusted-close series is used as an investable short-duration cash
proxy. It is not a frictionless risk-free asset.

\subsection{System controls}

The implementation separates data, alpha, portfolio, timing, risk, backtest,
execution, and strategy layers under one Python package. Allocation contracts
are strict at strategy output and relaxed only when an overlay intentionally
reduces gross exposure. Point-in-time merge guards reject fundamentals that
were not yet announced. Optional broker, machine-learning, and provider SDKs
remain lazy imports so the offline scientific core is testable without
credentials.

Research outputs include the generator hash, Git revision, Python and package
versions, input SHA-256 digest, full experiment design, and hashes of every
table and figure. Paper-trading state is stored as one versioned snapshot of
positions, intents, orders, and metadata; writes are atomic and use optimistic
concurrency. An order intent is persisted before submission, and an uncertain
submission is not retried automatically. Broker conformance tests instantiate
the current Alpaca and Interactive Brokers SDK request types, but authenticated
transport and venue behavior remain outside this study.

\section{Case study and experimental design}

\subsection{Fixed strategy}

The universe contains QQQ, VGT, GLD, TLT, SPY, and VIG, grouped as growth,
real assets, and defensive proxies. On the first session of each week, the
strategy ranks groups by a 126-session information ratio relative to QQQ and
keeps the top two. Within selected groups, it estimates a one-factor regression
on a preceding 126-session window and sums residual returns over a separate
126-session formation window, skipping the latest 21 sessions. Positive scores
receive proportional long-only weights, capped at 60\% per asset.
QQQ is also one of the two growth-group members, so that group's active-return
spread contains one zero benchmark leg; this asymmetric product specification
is held fixed.

Two exposure overlays are evaluated. A seeded three-state Gaussian-mixture
regime model maps QQQ return and realized-volatility features to zero, half, or
full exposure. A volatility scaler targets 20 annualized volatility points; no
VIX series is supplied, so the experiment uses the documented 21-session QQQ
realized-volatility proxy. The full strategy uses both overlays. Three
predefined variants remove the regime gate, remove the volatility scaler, or
remove both. This audit did not tune parameters or select a variant based on
the reported period. The unknown number of configurations explored before the
audit prevents a defensible multiple-testing-adjusted discovery claim.

\subsection{Data, comparators, and uncertainty}

The owner-supplied matrix contains adjusted closes from 2016-01-04 through
2025-12-31, retrieved on 2026-06-16 through yfinance 1.4.1 with
\texttt{auto\_adjust=False}. The yfinance documentation describes the Yahoo
Finance API as intended for personal use and directs users to Yahoo's terms of
use.\footnote{\url{https://ranaroussi.github.io/yfinance/}} The repository owner
attests permission to publish the derived aggregate results; the raw matrix is
not distributed. Its SHA-256 is:
\begin{center}
  \small\texttt{efb384a62157e56a0cd8065abf45c1ed07d90ec26c681e5d54d74fe4cb9c55e1}
\end{center}
The first 503 sessions are signal history; evaluation covers 2,011 sessions
from 2018-01-02 through 2025-12-31.

Comparators share one capital clock. SPY and a six-ETF basket initialized at
equal dollar weights are measured from the close immediately preceding the
evaluation window. To isolate cross-sectional decisions from exposure timing,
the basket return is multiplied by the strategy's realized risky exposure each
day and the remainder earns SHV. This comparator is gross of its own trading
costs. We therefore report both gross-to-gross signal comparisons and the more
conservative net-strategy-to-gross-benchmark comparison.

For dependent daily observations, we resample paired active-return series with
the circular-block bootstrap of \citet{politis1992circular}. We use 5,000
replications, 21-session blocks, and seed 42. Intervals quantify sampling
variation conditional on this one historical path, strategy specification,
cash proxy, and block length; they do not establish stationarity or future
performance.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Cash accounting changes the reported level, not the verdict}

Table~\ref{tab:accounting} and Figure~\ref{fig:accounting} separate cash and
cost effects. Crediting residual cash raises net CAGR from -0.07\% under a
zero-return cash assumption to 1.33\%. That correction is economically
material because mean risky exposure is only 34.76\% and the strategy is fully
in cash on 41.92\% of sessions. It does not create evidence of alpha: SHV alone
compounds at 2.50\%, and the exposure-matched passive basket compounds at
6.54\%.

\subsection{Costs and overlays do not rescue the strategy}

The strategy turns over 10.84 times per year on a one-way basis. At zero cost,
its cash-excess Sharpe is 0.15; it declines to 0.03 at 5 bps, -0.14 at the
13-bps baseline, -0.41 at 25 bps, and -0.94 at 50 bps. The model is therefore
not economically robust to modest execution friction.

Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} compares each variant before costs with a passive
basket at the same exposure. This avoids attributing the result solely to
strategy turnover. The full model has gross cash-excess Sharpe 0.15 versus
0.69 for its comparator. Removing the regime gate, the volatility scaler, or
both increases strategy exposure and raw returns, but every gross strategy
still trails its matched comparator. The corresponding annualized gross active
returns are -3.08\%, -4.63\%, -3.21\%, and -4.28\%. No variant is selected as a
replacement model.

\subsection{Uncertainty, subperiods, and engine parity}

The net strategy's annualized arithmetic excess return over SHV is -0.94\%,
with a 95\% block-bootstrap interval of [-5.27\%, 3.55\%]. Before costs, the
corresponding estimate is 0.95\% [-3.31\%, 5.40\%]. Neither supports a positive
cash-relative claim. Relative to the exposure-matched basket, gross active
return is -3.08\% [-5.58\%, -0.56\%], and only 0.64\% of bootstrap replications
are positive. After costs it is -4.97\% [-7.58\%, -2.39\%], with no positive
replication among 5,000 draws.

The conclusion also holds in both prespecified halves. In 2018--2021, strategy
CAGR is 0.08\% and cash-excess Sharpe is -0.18, versus 4.77\% and 0.78 for the
matched basket. In 2022--2025, the values are 2.59\% and -0.12, versus 8.34\%
and 0.66. Finally, the vectorized and share-based event-driven engines produce
net CAGRs of 1.325\% and 1.328\%, respectively, and cash-excess Sharpes of
-0.1442 and -0.1440. This close agreement is a diagnostic for this experiment,
not a claim that their rebalancing semantics are generally equivalent.

\section{Limitations and broader impact}

This study evaluates one U.S.-listed ETF universe over one eight-year window.
SHV has duration, expenses, and market-price variation, so it is only a cash
proxy. Adjusted closes omit intraday spreads and fill uncertainty. The flat
cost grid excludes order-size, volatility, venue, and market-impact dynamics.
The exposure-matched basket is a diagnostic comparator, not a directly quoted
security, and is reported gross of its own rebalancing cost. The bootstrap is
conditional on a weak-dependence approximation and a chosen 21-session block.
Historical research trial counts are unknown, so a Deflated Sharpe Ratio would
require an assumed rather than observed number of trials. The data source can
revise adjusted histories; the exact input is identified by digest but is not
openly redistributed. No live broker authentication, exchange acknowledgment,
or funded paper order was tested.

The positive impact of an audit-first protocol is reduced false confidence:
cash, costs, timing, and negative results become visible rather than optimized
away. The principal risk is the opposite misuse: readers may treat an audited
backtest as investment advice or deployment approval. The repository therefore
defaults to paper mode, requires pre-trade risk checks, blocks uncertain order
retries, and describes the strategy as a research baseline. Past simulated
performance does not imply future returns.

\section{Conclusion}

Software correctness and investment efficacy are different hypotheses. In
this case, explicit cash accounting materially improves the reported CAGR,
while matched-exposure benchmarks, gross-to-gross ablations, and dependence-
aware uncertainty show that the fixed strategy does not add value over simple
passive exposure. The broader lesson is procedural: weight-centric trading
research should make cash a first-class asset, separate gross signal from net
implementation, compare like exposure with like exposure, preserve every
predefined variant, and bind artifacts to code and inputs. A negative result
under those controls is more useful than a stronger chart built on ambiguous
accounting.

\section{Reproducibility details}

The complete generator is \texttt{scripts/run\_paper\_experiments.py}. From a
locked development environment, the fixed command is:
\begin{verbatim}
PYTHONPATH=. .venv/bin/python scripts/run_paper_experiments.py \
  --prices-csv local_data/published_rotation_prices.csv \
  --output-dir paper --bootstrap-replications 5000
\end{verbatim}
The input must contain adjusted-close columns QQQ, VGT, GLD, TLT, SPY, VIG,
and SHV and match the published digest for exact reproduction. Aggregate CSVs,
figures, and their hashes are in \texttt{paper/results/manifest.json}. The run
used Python 3.14.4 on an eight-core Apple M1 with 8 GiB RAM and completed in
approximately 25 seconds on CPU; no GPU was used. Supported CI runs use Python
3.11 through 3.14. The reported experiment consumed less than one CPU-minute;
repository-wide testing, packaging, notebook execution, and broker conformance
checks were additional engineering validation rather than model search.
